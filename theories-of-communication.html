1. Introduction

Theories are the lenses through which we make sense of the world. In the study of organisational communication, theories provide structured explanations of how messages are created, transmitted, received, and interpreted — and why these processes sometimes succeed brilliantly and sometimes fail disastrously. Without theoretical frameworks, our understanding of communication would remain at the level of common sense and personal anecdote. Theories allow us to move beyond "I think communication is important" to a deeper understanding of why it is important, how it works, and what can be done when it breaks down.

In Topics 1 and 2, we defined communication, examined the communication process, and explored the channels through which messages travel. This lecture builds on that foundation by introducing the major theoretical frameworks that scholars have developed to explain communication. These theories range from early linear models that treated communication as a simple one-way transmission, through interactive models that introduced the concept of feedback, to transactional models that recognise communication as a dynamic, simultaneous, meaning-making process. We also examine how broader organisational theories — classical, human relations, and systems approaches — shape the way communication is understood and practised within organisations.

Nwogbaga, Nwankwo, and Onwe (2023) emphasised that understanding communication theories is not an abstract academic exercise — it is a practical necessity for anyone who wants to diagnose communication problems, design effective communication systems, and lead organisations where information flows accurately, efficiently, and equitably.

2. Why Study Communication Theories?

Students sometimes ask why they need to study theories when they are already communicating every day. The answer lies in the difference between doing something and understanding it. A person can drive a car without understanding how the engine works — but a mechanic who understands the engine can diagnose problems, make repairs, and improve performance in ways that the ordinary driver cannot. Similarly, a person who understands communication theories can analyse, diagnose, and improve communication in ways that go far beyond everyday intuition.

Key Concept — The Value of Communication Theories

Communication theories serve several essential purposes for students and practitioners:

1. Explanation: Theories explain why communication works the way it does. Why do some messages succeed while others fail? Why is face-to-face communication richer than email? Why do rumours spread faster than official announcements? Theories provide answers grounded in systematic observation and analysis rather than guesswork.

2. Prediction: Once we understand how communication works, we can predict what is likely to happen in given circumstances. If a manager sends a complex, emotionally sensitive message through a lean channel (email), theory predicts a higher probability of misunderstanding. This predictive power allows communicators to anticipate problems and prevent them.

3. Diagnosis: When communication fails, theories help us identify where in the process the breakdown occurred. Was the message poorly encoded? Was the channel inappropriate? Was there noise? Was feedback absent? Different theories highlight different potential points of failure.

4. Improvement: Theories provide the basis for designing better communication systems, policies, and practices. An organisation that understands systems theory, for instance, will recognise the importance of feedback loops and interdepartmental communication in ways that an organisation operating purely on intuition will not.

5. Critical evaluation: Theories give students a vocabulary and framework for critically evaluating communication practices — in their workplaces, in the media, and in public discourse. This critical capacity is an essential skill for any educated professional.

3. Linear Models of Communication

The earliest formal theories of communication treated the process as a linear, one-way transmission from a sender to a receiver. These models, developed in the mid-twentieth century, were heavily influenced by engineering and telecommunications — fields concerned with the efficient transmission of signals from one point to another. While they have been superseded by more sophisticated models, linear theories remain important because they introduced foundational concepts — sender, message, channel, receiver, noise — that are still used today.

3.1 The Shannon-Weaver Model (1949)

Key Concept — The Shannon-Weaver Model

The Shannon-Weaver Model, originally developed for telecommunications engineering, is widely regarded as the first formal model of communication. It describes communication as a linear process with five components:

1. Information source (Sender): The person or device that originates the message.

2. Transmitter (Encoder): The mechanism that converts the message into a signal suitable for the channel — in human communication, this is the process of putting thoughts into words, gestures, or written text.

3. Channel: The medium through which the signal travels — air (for speech), paper (for writing), electronic networks (for digital messages).

4. Receiver (Decoder): The mechanism that converts the signal back into a message the destination can understand — in human communication, this is the process of listening, reading, or observing.

5. Destination: The person or device for whom the message is intended.

The model also introduced the critically important concept of noise — any interference that distorts the signal between transmitter and receiver. In engineering, noise referred to static on a telephone line; in organisational communication, noise encompasses anything that disrupts the message — physical noise, semantic confusion, psychological barriers, or organisational filtering.

Key Concept — Strengths and Limitations of the Shannon-Weaver Model

Strengths: The model introduced a clear, systematic vocabulary for discussing communication. The concepts of sender, receiver, channel, encoding, decoding, and noise remain foundational to all subsequent models. It drew attention to the fact that communication can fail — that noise exists and must be managed — which was a powerful insight for organisations accustomed to assuming that sent messages were automatically received and understood.

Limitations: The model is linear and one-directional — it depicts communication as flowing from sender to receiver with no feedback mechanism. In reality, human communication is interactive and reciprocal. The model also treats the receiver as passive — a destination that simply absorbs the message — ignoring the active role that receivers play in interpreting, questioning, and responding. Furthermore, it was designed for technical signal transmission, not for the complex, meaning-laden, emotionally rich communication that characterises human interaction in organisations. Ishii, Lyons, and Carr (2020) noted that while the Shannon-Weaver model remains useful as a starting point, it is insufficient for understanding the full complexity of modern organisational communication.

Workplace Example — Shannon-Weaver in an Organisation

A managing director (information source) dictates a memo (encoding) about a new overtime policy. The memo is printed and distributed via internal mail (channel). Employees (receivers/destinations) read and interpret the memo (decoding). However, the photocopier produced blurry copies on some pages (noise — physical), and several employees misunderstood the term "compensatory time off" because it was not defined (noise — semantic). The Shannon-Weaver model helps us identify that the communication failed at two points: the channel introduced physical noise, and the encoding introduced semantic noise. However, the model does not account for what happened next — employees talking among themselves to try to figure out the policy, or the frustration they felt at not being consulted. These interactive and emotional dimensions require more advanced models.

3.2 Berlo's SMCR Model (1960)

Key Concept — Berlo's SMCR Model

David Berlo expanded the linear approach by proposing the SMCR Model, which stands for Source, Message, Channel, Receiver. Berlo's key contribution was identifying the factors that influence the effectiveness of each element:

Source factors: The sender's communication skills (speaking, writing, listening), attitudes (towards self, subject, and receiver), knowledge level, social system, and cultural context all affect how effectively the message is encoded.

Message factors: The content, structure, code (language), and treatment (how the message is presented — formally, informally, persuasively, neutrally) all affect how the message is received.

Channel factors: The five senses — seeing, hearing, touching, smelling, tasting — serve as channels through which messages are received. In organisational communication, this translates to the choice of visual, auditory, or written media.

Receiver factors: The receiver's communication skills, attitudes, knowledge, social system, and cultural context all affect how the message is decoded and interpreted. Crucially, Berlo recognised that the receiver is not a blank slate — the same message can be interpreted very differently by different receivers depending on their personal characteristics.

Strength: Berlo's model drew attention to the human factors — skills, attitudes, knowledge, culture — that influence communication at every stage. This was a significant advancement over the purely mechanical Shannon-Weaver model.

Limitation: Like Shannon-Weaver, Berlo's model remains essentially linear — it does not include feedback and still depicts communication as a one-way process from source to receiver.

Workplace Example — Berlo's SMCR in Practice

A Nigerian branch manager (source) delivers a training presentation about a new customer service protocol to a mixed team that includes both experienced staff and newly recruited graduates (receivers). The branch manager has strong presentation skills and deep knowledge of the protocol (source factors favour success). However, the presentation uses heavy technical jargon (message factor — code) that the new recruits do not yet understand. The experienced staff follow easily, but the new recruits leave the session confused and anxious. Berlo's model explains the failure: the source did not adequately consider the receivers' knowledge level when encoding the message. The content was correct, the channel was appropriate (face-to-face), but the mismatch between message code and receiver knowledge undermined the communication. The solution: adapt the language to the least experienced members of the audience, or provide a glossary of terms before the session.

4. Interactive Models of Communication

The limitations of linear models — particularly the absence of feedback — led to the development of interactive models that recognised communication as a two-way process. Interactive models introduced the concept of feedback and acknowledged that the receiver is an active participant, not a passive recipient.

4.1 Osgood-Schramm Model (1954)

Key Concept — The Osgood-Schramm Circular Model

The Osgood-Schramm Model represented a major departure from linear thinking. It depicted communication as a circular, reciprocal process in which both parties function simultaneously as senders and receivers. The model emphasises three key activities that each party performs continuously:

1. Encoding: Converting thoughts into a transmittable message.

2. Decoding: Interpreting the received message and assigning meaning to it.

3. Interpreting: Making sense of the decoded message in light of one's own knowledge, experience, beliefs, and context.

The circular design of the model shows that communication has no fixed starting or ending point — both parties are constantly encoding, decoding, and interpreting in a continuous loop. When Person A speaks, Person B listens, interprets, and responds — and that response becomes a new message that Person A must decode and interpret.

The model also introduced the concept of "field of experience" — the idea that effective communication depends on the degree of overlap between the sender's and receiver's experiences, knowledge, vocabulary, and cultural backgrounds. Where the fields overlap, shared meaning is possible. Where they do not overlap, misunderstanding is likely. Nwogbaga, Nwankwo, and Onwe (2023) noted that this concept is particularly relevant in multicultural organisations where employees from different backgrounds may have limited overlapping fields of experience.

Strength: The model correctly identifies communication as interactive and reciprocal, and the "field of experience" concept is powerful and practical.

Limitation: The model depicts communication as a sequential, turn-taking process — one person speaks, then the other responds. In reality, communication is often simultaneous: while one person is speaking, the other is already reacting non-verbally (nodding, frowning, leaning forward) and formulating a response. This limitation was addressed by transactional models.

Workplace Example — Field of Experience

An expatriate manager from Germany is posted to a Nigerian subsidiary. During her first team meeting, she asks employees to "be direct and tell me what's not working." In German professional culture, direct feedback is valued and expected. However, several Nigerian team members — socialised in a culture that places high value on respect for authority and indirect communication — interpret the request as a test and remain silent, not wanting to appear disrespectful or confrontational. The communication fails not because the words were unclear, but because the fields of experience — cultural norms around directness, authority, and feedback — did not overlap sufficiently. Understanding this gap is the first step towards bridging it.

5. Transactional Models of Communication

Transactional models represent the most advanced understanding of the communication process. They build on interactive models by recognising that communication is not merely a back-and-forth exchange of messages but a simultaneous, dynamic process in which all parties are continuously and simultaneously sending and receiving messages, creating shared meaning in real time.

5.1 Barnlund's Transactional Model (1970)

Key Concept — Barnlund's Transactional Model

Dean Barnlund's Transactional Model made several groundbreaking contributions to our understanding of communication:

1. Simultaneity: Communication is not sequential (I speak, then you speak). Both parties are sending and receiving messages at the same time. While a manager is explaining a new policy, the employees are simultaneously communicating through their facial expressions, body posture, note-taking behaviour, and eye contact — all of which the manager is receiving and interpreting even as she continues speaking.

2. Co-creation of meaning: Meaning is not simply transmitted from sender to receiver like a package. It is co-created by both parties through their interaction. The meaning of a message emerges from the dynamic interplay between what the sender intends and what the receiver interprets. This means that communication is inherently collaborative — both parties contribute to the outcome.

3. Context dependence: Communication is always embedded in a context — physical (the room, the noise level, the seating arrangement), social (the relationship between the parties, their relative status), cultural (their shared and divergent norms), and temporal (the history of their previous interactions). The same words spoken in different contexts can carry entirely different meanings.

4. Both parties are "communicators": The model abandons the terms "sender" and "receiver" in favour of "communicators," reflecting the fact that both parties are performing both functions simultaneously. This shift in language reflects a profound shift in understanding: communication is not something one person does to another — it is something people do with each other.

Yue, Men, and Ferguson (2021) applied transactional principles to internal organisational communication, demonstrating that organisations where leaders treat communication as a two-way, co-creative process — rather than a one-way broadcast — achieve higher levels of employee engagement, trust, and organisational identification.

Workplace Example — Transactional Communication

During a performance review meeting, a supervisor (Communicator A) is giving feedback to an employee (Communicator B) about missed deadlines. As the supervisor speaks, the employee's face shows visible anxiety — eyes widening, arms crossing, posture stiffening. The supervisor reads these non-verbal signals simultaneously and adjusts her approach in real time — softening her tone, adding a reassuring statement, and pausing to ask how the employee is feeling. The employee, sensing the shift in tone, relaxes slightly and begins to explain the workload issues that caused the delays. The meaning of the interaction — its emotional tone, its outcomes, its impact on the working relationship — was not determined by the supervisor's prepared feedback alone. It was co-created through the dynamic, simultaneous exchange of verbal and non-verbal messages by both parties. This is transactional communication in action.

6. Classical Organisational Theory and Communication

Communication does not exist in a vacuum within organisations — it is shaped by the organisation's structure, culture, and management philosophy. Different organisational theories imply different approaches to communication. Understanding these connections helps students see how communication practices are embedded in broader organisational frameworks.

Key Concept — Classical Theory and Communication

Classical organisational theory, associated with thinkers such as Frederick Taylor (Scientific Management), Henri Fayol (Administrative Management), and Max Weber (Bureaucratic Theory), viewed organisations as rational, mechanistic systems designed for maximum efficiency. In this framework, communication serves a primarily regulatory and directive function:

Communication is top-down: Orders, instructions, and policies flow downward from management to workers. Upward communication is limited to formal reports and requests for approval.

Communication is formal: Written rules, procedures, memos, and standardised forms dominate. Informal communication is discouraged or ignored.

Communication is task-oriented: The purpose of communication is to direct work, enforce compliance, and maintain control. Social, relational, and emotional communication are considered irrelevant or even counterproductive.

Communication follows the chain of command: Messages must travel through the official hierarchy — an employee communicates with their supervisor, who communicates with the department head, who communicates with the director. Bypassing the chain of command is considered a violation of organisational discipline.

Strengths in practice: Classical communication practices provide clarity, consistency, and accountability. Everyone knows who reports to whom, what channels to use, and what documentation is required. In large, complex organisations — military organisations, government bureaucracies, manufacturing plants — this structure is essential for maintaining order and coordination.

Weaknesses in practice: Classical communication is rigid, slow, and one-directional. It discourages upward feedback, stifles innovation, and ignores the social and emotional dimensions of workplace interaction. Mbarushimana and Allida (2022) found that organisations that rely exclusively on classical communication structures experience higher levels of information filtering, employee disengagement, and resistance to change.

7. Human Relations Theory and Communication

Key Concept — Human Relations Theory and Communication

The Human Relations movement, which emerged from the Hawthorne Studies of the 1920s and 1930s and was developed by scholars such as Elton Mayo, Abraham Maslow, and Douglas McGregor, challenged the classical view by arguing that organisations are fundamentally social systems — that employees are human beings with emotional needs, social relationships, and personal motivations that profoundly influence their work behaviour.

Human relations theory transformed the understanding of organisational communication in several important ways:

Communication is multi-directional: Not only downward, but also upward (employee voice, feedback, suggestions) and horizontal (peer-to-peer collaboration, informal interaction). Effective organisations actively encourage communication in all directions.

Informal communication is valued: Rather than dismissing the grapevine as a nuisance, human relations theory recognised informal networks as vital sources of social support, information, and organisational intelligence.

Communication is relational: The quality of workplace relationships — trust between managers and employees, camaraderie among colleagues, a sense of belonging — is directly linked to communication effectiveness. Employees who feel heard, respected, and valued communicate more openly and honestly.

Listening is as important as speaking: Human relations theory elevated the role of the listener. Managers were encouraged to practise active listening, show empathy, and create environments where employees felt safe to express their ideas and concerns.

Men and Yue (2024) demonstrated that human relations principles remain highly relevant in contemporary organisations: their research found that transparent, empathetic leadership communication is the single most powerful predictor of employee trust and organisational commitment.

Workplace Example — Classical vs Human Relations

Classical approach: A factory manager discovers that productivity has declined on the night shift. He issues a formal memo warning employees that further declines will result in disciplinary action. Communication is one-way, top-down, and punitive.

Human relations approach: The same factory manager holds an informal meeting with night shift workers, asks them to share their challenges, listens actively, and discovers that the decline is caused by a malfunctioning ventilation system that makes working conditions uncomfortable. He addresses the environmental issue and thanks the workers for their honesty. Communication is two-way, respectful, and problem-solving oriented.

The outcome: the classical approach may produce short-term compliance through fear, but it fails to identify and solve the actual problem. The human relations approach solves the problem, builds trust, and creates a precedent for honest upward communication in the future.

8. Systems Theory and Communication

Key Concept — Systems Theory and Communication

Systems theory views the organisation as an open, interconnected system composed of interdependent parts (subsystems) that interact with each other and with the external environment. Communication, in this framework, is the mechanism that connects the parts and enables the system to function as a coherent whole.

Several key systems concepts are directly relevant to organisational communication:

1. Interdependence: Every part of the organisation is connected to every other part. A change in one department — a new policy, a budget cut, a personnel change — affects other departments. Communication is the process through which these interconnections are managed. Without effective communication, changes in one subsystem create unintended disruptions in others.

2. Feedback loops: Systems theory places enormous emphasis on feedback — the process through which the output of a system is fed back into the system as input, enabling self-correction and adaptation. In organisations, feedback loops include performance reviews, customer surveys, employee suggestion schemes, quality control data, and financial reports. Organisations with strong feedback loops can identify problems early and adapt quickly; organisations with weak feedback loops are slow to recognise and respond to changing conditions.

3. Openness: Organisations are open systems that interact with their external environment — receiving inputs (resources, information, demands) and producing outputs (products, services, communications). External communication — with customers, regulators, competitors, and the public — is essential for organisational survival and adaptation.

4. Equifinality: There is more than one way to achieve the same goal. Different communication strategies, channels, and structures may be equally effective depending on the context. Systems theory encourages flexibility and adaptability rather than rigid adherence to a single communication approach.

5. Entropy: Without energy and information, systems tend towards disorder (entropy). In organisations, communication is the force that counteracts entropy — it maintains order, coordination, and shared understanding. When communication breaks down, the organisation moves towards disorder: misaligned goals, duplicated efforts, conflicting actions, and declining performance.

Neuberger, Lemos, and Watkins (2023) argued that systems theory provides the most comprehensive framework for understanding communication in today's complex, networked, digitally connected organisations, where information flows rapidly across internal and external boundaries in ways that linear and even interactive models cannot adequately capture.

Workplace Example — Systems Theory in Action

A university introduces a new online registration system. The IT department develops the software (subsystem 1), the registrar's office defines the registration rules (subsystem 2), student affairs trains students to use the system (subsystem 3), and the finance department links it to fee payment (subsystem 4). During the first registration period, the system crashes repeatedly because the IT department was not told about the peak load expectations (communication failure between subsystems 2 and 1), students are confused because the training materials do not match the final interface (communication failure between subsystems 1 and 3), and fee payments fail because the finance integration was completed late (communication failure between subsystems 1 and 4). Systems theory explains that the failures were not caused by any single department's incompetence — they were caused by inadequate communication between interdependent subsystems. The solution is not to blame individual departments but to establish stronger cross-functional communication channels and feedback loops.

9. Contemporary Perspectives

While the foundational theories discussed above remain relevant, contemporary scholars have extended and enriched our understanding of organisational communication in response to the realities of the twenty-first-century workplace — globalisation, digital technology, remote work, cultural diversity, and the increasing pace of change.

9.1 The Transparent Communication Theory

Key Concept — Transparent Communication

Transparent communication theory proposes that organisational effectiveness is maximised when leaders communicate openly, honestly, and proactively with employees — sharing not only good news but also challenges, uncertainties, and the reasoning behind decisions. Transparency builds trust, reduces rumour and speculation, and empowers employees to make informed contributions.

Men and Yue (2024) identified three dimensions of transparent communication: informational transparency (sharing substantive, relevant information promptly), participatory transparency (involving employees in decision-making processes), and accountability transparency (taking responsibility for organisational actions and outcomes). Their research demonstrated that leaders who practise all three dimensions build significantly higher levels of employee trust and commitment than those who practise only one or two.

9.2 Media Richness Theory Revisited

Key Concept — Media Richness in the Digital Age

As discussed in Topic 2, Media Richness Theory proposes that communication effectiveness depends on matching the richness of the medium to the complexity of the message. Ishii, Lyons, and Carr (2020) revisited this theory for the contemporary digital workplace, confirming its core principle while noting that modern tools like video conferencing have created new "middle ground" media that are richer than text but leaner than face-to-face interaction. They argued that effective digital communicators must develop media competence — the ability to select, combine, and adapt digital tools to match the communication demands of each situation.

9.3 Communication Privacy Management Theory

Key Concept — Communication Privacy Management (CPM)

Communication Privacy Management Theory addresses how people manage the boundaries between public and private information. In organisational settings, this theory is increasingly relevant as digital communication blurs the boundaries between work and personal life, and as organisations collect, store, and share growing volumes of employee and customer data.

CPM theory proposes that individuals develop privacy rules — personal guidelines about what information to share, with whom, through which channels, and under what circumstances. When these rules are violated — when a manager shares an employee's personal health information without consent, or when a company-wide email accidentally reveals salary data — the resulting "boundary turbulence" can damage trust, relationships, and organisational culture. Ruck and Welch (2022) emphasised that organisations must establish clear privacy policies and communication norms that respect individual boundaries while enabling the information sharing necessary for organisational functioning.

10. Comparing the Theories

Each theory introduced in this lecture highlights different aspects of the communication process and offers different insights for organisational practice. No single theory provides a complete picture — together, they offer a comprehensive toolkit for understanding, analysing, and improving communication in any organisational setting.

Key Concept — Comparative Summary

Linear models (Shannon-Weaver, Berlo) are useful for understanding the basic mechanics of message transmission and for identifying sources of noise, but they fail to capture the interactive, reciprocal nature of human communication.

Interactive models (Osgood-Schramm) correctly introduce feedback and the concept of shared fields of experience, but they still depict communication as a sequential, turn-taking process rather than a simultaneous one.

Transactional models (Barnlund) provide the most accurate representation of how communication actually works — as a simultaneous, dynamic, context-dependent process of co-creating meaning — but they are more complex and harder to apply to specific diagnostic situations.

Classical organisational theory explains why many organisations default to formal, top-down, task-oriented communication — and why this approach, while orderly, often leads to filtering, rigidity, and disengagement.

Human relations theory highlights the importance of relational, empathetic, multi-directional communication for employee motivation, trust, and morale.

Systems theory provides the most holistic framework, emphasising interdependence, feedback loops, openness, and the role of communication in maintaining organisational coherence in complex environments.

Contemporary theories (transparent communication, media richness revisited, CPM) address the specific challenges of the digital age — information overload, privacy, trust, and the need for media competence.

The effective organisational communicator draws on multiple theories as needed — using linear models to diagnose channel problems, transactional models to understand interpersonal dynamics, systems theory to design cross-functional communication structures, and contemporary theories to navigate digital and cultural complexity.

11. Examination Practice Questions

Examination Practice — Attempt All Questions

Answer in clear, well-structured prose with relevant examples where appropriate. Marks are allocated to each question as indicated.

  1. Discuss the Shannon-Weaver model of communication. In your answer, identify and explain each element of the model, discuss the concept of noise, and evaluate the model's strengths and limitations for understanding communication in organisations. (20 marks)
  2. Enumerate and discuss the four factors that influence each element of Berlo's SMCR model. Use a workplace scenario to illustrate how a mismatch between source factors and receiver factors can lead to communication failure. (20 marks)
  3. The Osgood-Schramm model introduced the concept of "field of experience." Discuss what this concept means, why it is important for organisational communication, and how a manager can address situations where communicators' fields of experience do not overlap sufficiently. (15 marks)
  4. Barnlund's transactional model describes communication as simultaneous rather than sequential. Discuss the key principles of the transactional model and explain, with a workplace example, how it provides a more accurate picture of communication than linear models. (15 marks)
  5. List and discuss three differences between classical organisational theory and human relations theory in their approach to communication. In your opinion, which approach is more suitable for a modern organisation? Justify your position with relevant arguments. (20 marks)
  6. Discuss the contribution of systems theory to our understanding of organisational communication. In your answer, explain at least three key systems concepts (such as interdependence, feedback loops, and entropy) and show how each relates to communication practice. (20 marks)
  7. Transparent communication is increasingly recognised as essential for organisational effectiveness. Enumerate and discuss the three dimensions of transparent communication identified in contemporary research. For each dimension, provide a practical example of how it could be applied in a workplace setting. (15 marks)
  8. Communication theories have evolved from linear models to transactional models over several decades. Discuss this evolution, explaining what each stage contributed and what limitation it addressed from the previous stage. (20 marks)
  9. In your opinion, is it possible for a single theory to fully explain how communication works in organisations, or is a combination of theories necessary? Justify your position with at least three well-developed arguments and reference to specific theories discussed in this lecture. (15 marks)
  10. Communication Privacy Management Theory addresses how individuals manage the boundary between public and private information. Discuss why this theory is particularly relevant in today's digitally connected workplaces. Provide at least two examples of how privacy boundary violations can damage organisational trust. (15 marks)
