Verbal Communication in the Organisation

Understanding how spoken and written words shape workplace interaction, decision-making, and organisational culture.

1. Introduction

Every organisation, regardless of its size, sector, or geographic location, depends on the effective use of words to function. From the morning briefing on a factory floor to the carefully drafted policy memo circulated across departments, verbal communication is the mechanism through which instructions are given, ideas are exchanged, conflicts are resolved, and relationships are built. Without it, coordination would collapse and organisational goals would remain abstract intentions rather than actionable plans.

Contemporary research continues to affirm the centrality of communication to organisational performance. Men and Yue (2024) demonstrated that transparent and consistent verbal communication from leadership significantly enhances employee trust and engagement, particularly during periods of organisational change. Similarly, Verčič and Špoljarić (2020) found that the quality of internal communication is a stronger predictor of employee satisfaction than compensation or physical working conditions. These findings confirm that verbal communication is not merely a routine organisational activity — it is a strategic resource that directly influences productivity, morale, and institutional stability.

This lecture explores verbal communication within the organisational context in detail. It defines the concept, distinguishes clearly between oral and written forms, examines the functions each form serves, identifies common barriers that impede effective communication, and offers practical strategies for overcoming those barriers. Throughout, emphasis is placed on providing clear conceptual explanations and real-world examples that will help students develop a thorough understanding of each lesson point.

2. Defining Verbal Communication

Verbal communication refers to the use of words — whether spoken or written — to convey meaning between individuals or groups. It is essential to note that the term "verbal" does not mean only "spoken," a common misconception among students. Verbal communication encompasses any message that relies on language, including both oral (spoken) and written forms. This distinguishes it from non-verbal communication, which relies on body language, facial expressions, gestures, tone of voice, spatial distance, and other cues that do not involve the use of words themselves.

Key Concept — Verbal Communication Defined

Verbal communication is the transmission of messages using words, either spoken (oral) or written. It is structured by the grammar, vocabulary, and conventions of a shared language, and it may be formal or informal depending on the organisational context.

The word "verbal" comes from the Latin verbum, meaning "word." Therefore, verbal communication means communication that uses words. This includes a spoken conversation between colleagues (oral verbal communication) as well as an email, report, or policy memo (written verbal communication). Both forms are verbal because they rely on language.

By contrast, a manager who frowns during a presentation or an employee who crosses their arms during a meeting is communicating non-verbally — without using words.

Within organisations, verbal communication operates along established channels — upward (from subordinates to superiors), downward (from superiors to subordinates), horizontal (between peers at the same level), and diagonal (across different levels and departments). It serves as the primary means through which policies are communicated, tasks are assigned, feedback is delivered, and conflicts are resolved. Understanding the distinction between oral and written verbal communication, and knowing when to use each, is a fundamental competency for anyone working in or managing an organisation.

3. Oral Communication in Organisations

Oral communication is the use of spoken words to exchange information. It is the most immediate and natural form of workplace communication, occurring in settings that range from informal hallway conversations to structured boardroom presentations. Ruck and Welch (2022) noted that oral communication remains the preferred method for conveying complex, sensitive, or emotionally charged information because it allows for instant feedback, vocal tone, and emotional nuance that written messages often cannot replicate.

Key Concept — Oral Communication

Oral communication is the process of expressing information, ideas, or feelings through spoken words. It requires the physical presence (or virtual connection) of at least two parties — a speaker and a listener — and allows for real-time interaction.

In organisational settings, oral communication is characterised by its immediacy (messages are delivered and received instantly), its interactivity (the listener can respond, ask questions, or seek clarification on the spot), and its richness (the speaker's tone, pace, emphasis, and vocal inflection add layers of meaning beyond the words themselves).

3.1 Common Forms of Oral Communication in the Workplace

Face-to-face conversations remain the richest communication medium available. According to the Media Richness Theory, originally proposed by Daft and Lengel and reaffirmed in contemporary research by Ishii, Lyons, and Carr (2020), face-to-face interaction permits the full range of verbal and non-verbal cues — words, tone, facial expression, gesture, and posture — making it the most effective medium for discussing complex or ambiguous topics.

Meetings — whether departmental, interdepartmental, or organisation-wide — provide a structured forum for discussion, decision-making, and collaborative problem-solving. Meetings can be formal (with set agendas, minutes, and scheduled times) or informal (spontaneous gatherings to address immediate concerns). Mroz, Allen, Verhoeven, and Shuffler (2023) found that the effectiveness of organisational meetings depends not merely on their frequency but on the clarity of their purpose and the quality of participation they encourage.

Presentations and briefings are used when a speaker must convey information to a larger audience in a planned, structured manner. These are common during project updates, training sessions, and strategic planning exercises.

Telephone and video calls have become increasingly important, particularly with the rise of remote and hybrid working arrangements. While they lack the full richness of in-person interaction, they offer greater immediacy and personal engagement than written alternatives.

Interviews, whether for recruitment, performance appraisal, or disciplinary purposes, represent a specialised form of oral communication that follows established protocols and carries significant organisational consequences.

Workplace Example

Consider a production manager at a manufacturing plant who notices a recurring quality control issue. Rather than sending an email that might be ignored, misunderstood, or deprioritised among dozens of other messages, she calls a brief team meeting on the factory floor. She explains the problem clearly, demonstrates the correct procedure using the actual equipment, takes questions from workers, and confirms understanding before the shift resumes. The immediacy, feedback, and personal engagement of this oral exchange make it far more effective than a written notice alone. This illustrates the principle that oral communication is best suited for situations requiring immediate action, clarification, and emotional engagement.

3.2 Advantages of Oral Communication

Oral communication offers several distinct advantages in the workplace. It provides immediate feedback, allowing speakers and listeners to clarify misunderstandings in real time without the delays inherent in written correspondence. It conveys emotional tone through vocal inflection, pacing, volume, and emphasis, adding layers of meaning that written text cannot replicate. It fosters personal connection and trust between colleagues, which Yue, Men, and Ferguson (2021) identified as essential for building employee engagement and commitment. Furthermore, oral communication is often faster than written alternatives, making it well suited for urgent instructions, time-sensitive decisions, or situations where speed matters more than documentation.

3.3 Limitations of Oral Communication

Despite its strengths, oral communication has notable limitations that must be understood. Spoken words leave no permanent record unless deliberately recorded or transcribed, which creates problems when accountability, legal compliance, or long-term documentation is required. Oral messages are also vulnerable to distortion as they pass through multiple levels of an organisation — a phenomenon known as the "serial transmission effect," where each person in the chain may alter, abbreviate, or embellish the original message. Verčič and Špoljarić (2020) noted that the longer the chain of oral transmission, the greater the likelihood that the original message will be significantly altered by the time it reaches its final recipient. Additionally, oral communication can be affected by speech impediments, accents, dialect differences, or environmental noise that may hinder clarity.

Key Concept — Serial Transmission Effect

The serial transmission effect (sometimes called the "Chinese whispers" effect) describes what happens when a message passes through several people in sequence. Each person in the chain unconsciously filters, simplifies, or reinterprets the message based on their own understanding, biases, and memory limitations. By the time the message reaches the final recipient, it may bear little resemblance to the original.

In organisations with tall hierarchies — many levels of management between the top executive and the front-line worker — this effect is particularly problematic. A directive from the managing director may pass through a general manager, a departmental head, a unit supervisor, and a team leader before reaching the worker. At each stage, the message risks losing detail, gaining unintended emphasis, or being reinterpreted. This is why many organisations supplement oral communication with written documentation to preserve the accuracy of important messages.

4. Written Communication in Organisations

Written communication involves the use of text to convey messages. In organisational settings, it serves as the backbone of formal documentation, policy dissemination, and institutional record-keeping. Unlike oral communication, written messages can be stored, retrieved, revised, duplicated, and distributed across time and distance with relative ease, making them indispensable for large and complex organisations.

Key Concept — Written Communication

Written communication is the process of conveying information, instructions, ideas, or decisions through text-based messages. These messages are recorded on a medium — paper, email, digital document, or instant messaging platform — and can be preserved for future reference.

Written communication is characterised by its permanence (messages can be stored and retrieved), its formality (it typically follows established conventions of grammar, structure, and format), and its deliberateness (the sender has time to compose, revise, and edit before transmitting). However, it lacks the immediacy and emotional richness of oral communication, and it relies entirely on the reader's ability to interpret meaning from words alone, without vocal tone or facial cues.

4.1 Common Forms of Written Communication

Written communication in the workplace includes memoranda (memos) for internal directives and announcements, reports for documenting findings, analyses, and recommendations, emails for day-to-day correspondence and coordination, letters for formal external communication with clients, suppliers, and regulatory bodies, policy manuals and handbooks for codifying organisational rules and procedures, and minutes of meetings for recording decisions and action items. In recent years, instant messaging platforms (such as Slack, Microsoft Teams, and WhatsApp Business) have introduced a semi-formal written channel that blends the speed of oral communication with the persistence of text.

4.2 Advantages of Written Communication

The most significant advantage of written communication is its permanence. A written directive, policy statement, or contract can be referenced months or years after it was issued, providing a reliable record for legal, administrative, or operational purposes. Written messages also allow for careful composition — the sender can draft, revise, and edit before transmitting, which reduces the likelihood of errors, poorly considered statements, or emotional outbursts that might occur in spontaneous oral exchanges. Furthermore, written communication enables wide distribution: a single memo or email can reach hundreds or thousands of employees simultaneously, ensuring that everyone receives the same information in the same words. Oduaran and Okojie (2023) emphasised that written communication is essential for maintaining consistency, standardisation, and institutional memory across large and geographically dispersed organisations.

4.3 Limitations of Written Communication

Written communication lacks the immediacy and emotional richness of face-to-face interaction. Tone is easily misinterpreted — a direct email, for instance, may be perceived as curt, aggressive, or dismissive when no such tone was intended by the sender. Written messages also require a baseline level of literacy and writing skill, and poorly constructed messages can cause confusion rather than clarity. There is also the growing problem of information overload: in many modern organisations, employees receive so many emails, reports, memos, and notifications that important messages may be overlooked, deprioritised, or simply unread. Welch (2022) reported that employees in large organisations receive an average of over 120 emails per day, making it increasingly difficult to distinguish critical communication from routine noise.

Workplace Example

A human resources department distributes a new leave policy via an internal memo and company-wide email. The written format ensures that every employee receives the same information and can refer back to it when needed. However, several employees misinterpret a clause about carryover leave because the language is ambiguous and legalistic. An oral briefing or Q&A session held alongside the written notice would have allowed the HR team to explain the clause in plain language, answer questions, and prevent confusion. This illustrates a critical principle: organisations often benefit most from combining written and oral approaches, using written communication for documentation and oral communication for explanation and clarification.

5. Functions of Verbal Communication in Organisations

Verbal communication serves multiple interconnected functions within an organisational setting. Understanding these functions helps students appreciate why communication is not merely an operational activity but a strategic organisational process. Drawing on contemporary scholarship, five primary functions can be identified:

Key Concept — The Five Functions

1. Informative function: The transmission of facts, data, instructions, and knowledge. This is the most fundamental function — ensuring that employees at every level have the information they need to perform their roles. Examples include work schedules, project updates, technical instructions, and performance data.

2. Regulatory function: The use of communication to direct, control, and standardise behaviour within the organisation. Policies, rules, codes of conduct, performance feedback, and managerial directives all serve a regulatory purpose. This function establishes expectations and boundaries.

3. Integrative function: The coordination of activities across different departments, teams, and hierarchical levels. Horizontal communication between peers and diagonal communication across boundaries serve this integrative role, ensuring that the separate parts of the organisation work towards shared goals rather than at cross-purposes.

4. Persuasive function: The use of verbal communication to influence attitudes, motivate action, negotiate outcomes, or build consensus. This function is especially visible in meetings, change-management initiatives, marketing communications, and leadership addresses. Men and Yue (2024) noted that persuasive internal communication is particularly critical during organisational transitions such as mergers, restructuring, or digital transformation.

5. Social and relational function: Beyond task-related purposes, verbal communication plays a vital role in building and maintaining workplace relationships. Casual conversations, expressions of appreciation, humour, and social exchanges contribute to organisational culture, group cohesion, and employee morale. Yue, Men, and Ferguson (2021) demonstrated that organisations where social communication is encouraged alongside task communication report higher levels of employee engagement and lower turnover.

6. Comparing Oral and Written Communication

A clear understanding of when to use oral versus written communication is an essential skill for effective organisational practice. The choice of medium should be guided by the nature of the message, the audience, the urgency of the situation, and the need for documentation. The following comparison highlights the key differences:

Key Concept — Oral vs Written: When to Use Each

Use oral communication when: the message is complex or ambiguous and requires discussion; the situation is emotionally sensitive (e.g., delivering bad news, resolving a conflict); immediate feedback is needed; building personal rapport or trust is important; or speed is more important than documentation.

Use written communication when: a permanent record is required for legal, regulatory, or accountability purposes; the message must reach a large, dispersed audience simultaneously; precision of wording matters (e.g., contracts, policies); the information is detailed or technical and the reader needs time to study it; or the message must be stored for future reference.

Use both together when: the message is important and must be both understood immediately and documented for future reference. For example, a new workplace safety procedure should be explained verbally in a training session and distributed in a written handbook. Ishii, Lyons, and Carr (2020) argued that the most effective organisational communicators are those who select the appropriate medium — or combination of media — based on a thoughtful analysis of the communication situation, not merely personal habit or convenience.

7. Barriers to Effective Verbal Communication

Effective verbal communication is not automatic. A range of barriers can distort, dilute, delay, or completely block messages as they travel between sender and receiver. Understanding these barriers is a prerequisite for overcoming them, and students should be able to identify, explain, and provide examples of each category.

Key Concept — Semantic Barriers

Semantic barriers arise when the words chosen by the sender carry different meanings for the receiver. Language is not always precise or universal — the same word or phrase can be interpreted differently depending on the receiver's education, profession, cultural background, or experience. Technical jargon, ambiguous phrasing, acronyms, and culturally specific idioms can all lead to misunderstanding.

Example: A software engineer tells a new administrative assistant to "push the update to production." The engineer means deploying code to a live server. The assistant, unfamiliar with technical terminology, is confused about what "push" and "production" mean in this context. The barrier here is not intelligence — it is the absence of a shared vocabulary.

Key Concept — Psychological Barriers

Psychological barriers include emotional states, attitudes, and cognitive biases that interfere with a person's ability to send or receive messages accurately. Anxiety, anger, distrust, prejudice, low self-esteem, and defensiveness can all distort how a message is composed, delivered, or interpreted.

Example: An employee who has recently received a poor performance review may interpret a routine email from her supervisor asking to "schedule a meeting" as a sign that she is about to be disciplined or dismissed. Her anxiety distorts a neutral message into a threatening one. The words have not changed, but her psychological state has altered their perceived meaning.

Key Concept — Physical Barriers

Physical barriers refer to environmental and technological factors that impede the transmission of verbal messages. These include noise, distance, poor acoustics, faulty equipment, unreliable internet connections, and uncomfortable physical settings.

Example: A video conference between team members in Lagos and Abuja is disrupted by poor internet connectivity. The audio cuts out repeatedly, key instructions are missed, and participants end the call with different understandings of what was agreed. The barrier is not human — it is technological and environmental.

Key Concept — Organisational Barriers

Organisational barriers stem from the structure, culture, and processes of the organisation itself. Tall hierarchies with many layers of management create opportunities for messages to be filtered, summarised, or distorted as they pass up or down the chain. Rigid departmental boundaries can discourage cross-functional communication. Centralised decision-making structures may suppress upward feedback. Mbarushimana and Allida (2022) identified information filtering — the tendency of employees to selectively edit messages to make them more acceptable to their superiors — as one of the most pervasive and damaging communication problems in hierarchical organisations.

Key Concept — Cultural and Linguistic Barriers

Cultural and linguistic barriers are particularly relevant in diverse, multinational, and multicultural organisations. Differences in language proficiency, communication norms, and cultural expectations regarding directness, formality, humour, and turn-taking can impede effective verbal exchange. What is considered polite directness in one culture may be perceived as rudeness in another; what is viewed as appropriate informality in one organisation may be seen as unprofessional in another.

Example: In a multinational corporation, a Japanese team member's silence during a meeting is interpreted by Western colleagues as disengagement or lack of ideas. In reality, the silence reflects a cultural norm of careful consideration before speaking — a sign of respect, not disinterest. Without cultural awareness, such differences become barriers rather than sources of strength.

Key Concept — Information Filtering

Information filtering occurs when a sender deliberately or unconsciously manipulates a message so that it appears more favourable, less threatening, or more aligned with what the receiver wants to hear. In organisations, employees commonly filter upward communication to avoid delivering bad news to superiors — a tendency that can have serious consequences for managerial decision-making.

Filtering is especially dangerous because it creates a false picture of reality at the top of the organisation. If managers only hear good news, they cannot identify problems, allocate resources correctly, or make informed strategic decisions. Mbarushimana and Allida (2022) stressed that creating a culture of psychological safety — where employees feel safe to speak honestly without fear of punishment — is the most effective long-term strategy for reducing information filtering.

8. Strategies for Improving Verbal Communication

Overcoming communication barriers requires deliberate, sustained effort from both individuals and organisations. The following strategies, supported by contemporary research and professional practice, can significantly enhance verbal communication effectiveness in the workplace.

Key Concept — Active Listening

Active listening is the practice of fully concentrating on, understanding, and responding to a speaker with deliberate attention and empathy. It goes beyond simply hearing words — it involves interpreting meaning, observing non-verbal cues, asking clarifying questions, and providing feedback that demonstrates understanding.

Active listening is foundational to effective verbal communication. Ruck and Welch (2022) emphasised that managers who practise active listening build stronger relationships with employees, identify problems earlier, and make better-informed decisions. In practice, active listening means maintaining eye contact, avoiding interruptions, paraphrasing key points to confirm understanding, and asking open-ended follow-up questions.

Clarity and simplicity in language should be a guiding principle. Communicators should avoid unnecessary jargon, use concrete rather than abstract terms, and structure messages logically with a clear beginning, middle, and end. This is especially important in written communication, where the reader cannot ask for immediate clarification. The question to ask before sending any message is: "Will the recipient understand this exactly as I intend it?"

Feedback mechanisms should be built into communication processes. Managers should regularly check for understanding — not by asking "Do you understand?" (which almost always invites a reflexive "yes") but by asking recipients to summarise or paraphrase the message in their own words. This technique, sometimes called the "tell-back method," reveals misunderstandings immediately and reinforces the message without creating an atmosphere of distrust.

Appropriate channel selection is critical. Not every message is suited to every medium. Ishii, Lyons, and Carr (2020) reaffirmed that rich media such as face-to-face conversation should be used for complex, ambiguous, or emotionally sensitive messages, while leaner media such as memos or emails are appropriate for routine, unambiguous, factual information. Choosing the wrong channel — such as delivering a redundancy notice by email, or announcing a major strategic change via a brief text message — can cause unnecessary confusion, anxiety, and resentment.

Organisational culture and psychological safety play a long-term role. Organisations that cultivate a culture of openness, mutual respect, and psychological safety tend to experience fewer communication breakdowns. When employees feel safe to speak honestly, raise concerns, and share bad news without fear of reprisal, the quality and accuracy of communication across all channels — upward, downward, horizontal, and diagonal — improves significantly. Men and Yue (2024) demonstrated that transparent leadership communication is the single most important factor in building this kind of communicative culture.

Practical Tip — The Tell-Back Method

When giving instructions to a team, try this simple technique: after explaining a task, ask a team member to describe the task back to you in their own words. Do not ask "Do you understand?" — most people will say yes regardless. Instead, say: "Before we start, could you walk me through what we've agreed so I can make sure I've explained it clearly?" Notice how this frames the check as a test of your explanation, not their comprehension — this protects the listener's dignity and encourages honest feedback. This small shift in language can dramatically reduce miscommunication in day-to-day operations.

9. Examination Practice Questions

Examination Practice — Attempt All Questions

The following questions are designed to test your understanding of verbal communication in organisations. Answer in clear, well-structured prose with relevant examples where appropriate. Marks are allocated to each question as indicated.

  1. Discuss the concept of verbal communication as it applies to organisational life. In your answer, distinguish clearly between oral and written forms, providing at least two workplace examples of each. (15 marks)
  2. Enumerate and discuss five functions that verbal communication serves in an organisational setting. Illustrate each function with a practical example drawn from a workplace scenario. (20 marks)
  3. List and discuss five barriers to effective verbal communication in organisations. For each barrier, explain its nature, provide a real-world example, and recommend one strategy an organisation could adopt to overcome it. (25 marks)
  4. Limitations of oral communication in an organisation are factors that hinder organisational growth. Discuss these limitations with relevant examples. In your opinion, under what circumstances should a manager prefer oral communication to written communication? (15 marks)
  5. Does oral or written communication play a more vital role in organisational productivity? With relevant examples, justify your position with at least three well-developed arguments. (15 marks)
  6. Enumerate and discuss four strategies for improving verbal communication effectiveness in the workplace. For each strategy, explain how it helps to overcome a specific communication barrier. (20 marks)
  7. Information filtering is a common problem in hierarchical organisations. Discuss what information filtering means, why it poses a danger to managerial decision-making, and what practical steps organisational leaders can take to minimise it. (15 marks)
  8. The rise of digital communication tools such as email, instant messaging, and video conferencing has transformed how organisations communicate. In your opinion, has this transformation been largely beneficial or harmful to organisational effectiveness? Discuss both the advantages and the challenges with relevant examples. (15 marks)
  9. List and discuss three forms of oral communication commonly used in the workplace. For each form, explain its purpose, describe a situation where it is most appropriate, and identify one potential limitation a manager should be aware of. (15 marks)
  10. Active listening is widely regarded as a critical skill for effective communication, yet many employees and managers struggle to practise it consistently. Discuss the concept of active listening, explain why it is often more difficult than it appears, and suggest three practical steps that individuals can take to develop this skill in the workplace. (15 marks)
