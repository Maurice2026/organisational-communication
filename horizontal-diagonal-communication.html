Section 1

Introduction

In the previous lecture, we examined vertical communication — the upward and downward flow of information along the organisational hierarchy. While vertical communication is essential for direction, instruction, and feedback, it represents only one dimension of communication within organisations. Equally important are the lateral and cross-cutting flows that occur when individuals and departments communicate with peers and with people outside their direct chain of command.

This lecture examines two such types: horizontal communication — the flow of messages between individuals or departments at the same hierarchical level — and diagonal communication — the flow of messages between individuals at different hierarchical levels and different departments simultaneously. Together, these forms of communication complete the picture of how information moves through any complex organisation.

🔑 Key Definitions

Horizontal Communication is the exchange of messages between individuals or units occupying the same level of the organisational hierarchy. Diagonal Communication is the exchange of messages between individuals at different hierarchical levels who belong to different departments or functional units — cutting across both rank and department simultaneously (Okafor, 2022).

The growing complexity of modern organisations has made both horizontal and diagonal communication increasingly critical. In an era of interdisciplinary teams, cross-functional projects, and digital collaboration, organisations that rely exclusively on vertical channels find themselves slow, siloed, and uncompetitive. Understanding how horizontal and diagonal communication work — and what impedes them — is therefore essential for any student of organisational communication (Eze & Nwachukwu, 2021).

Section 2

Understanding Horizontal Communication

Horizontal communication — also referred to as lateral communication — occurs when messages are exchanged between individuals, teams, or departments that occupy equivalent positions in the organisational hierarchy. Unlike vertical communication, horizontal communication does not cross authority boundaries; it occurs among equals, making it generally less formal and more collaborative in character.

Horizontal communication is the natural vehicle for peer coordination. It is through horizontal channels that departments with interdependent functions align their activities, share information, resolve operational conflicts, and pursue shared goals. Without it, each unit would be an isolated silo, dependent on the slow and circuitous route of communicating upward through the hierarchy and then back down into another department (Adesanya & Ibrahim, 2023).

Who Communicates Horizontally?

Horizontal communication occurs across all levels of an organisation — not merely at the top. Heads of department communicate horizontally with one another. Administrative officers at the same grade level coordinate tasks across departments. Lecturers from different faculties collaborate on interdisciplinary research. Students from different programmes work on joint projects. In each case, the defining feature is that the parties involved occupy equivalent positions, regardless of the specific level (Umoren & Uko, 2021).

Formal vs. Informal Horizontal Channels

Horizontal communication occurs through both formal and informal channels. Formal horizontal channels include officially sanctioned meetings, inter-departmental memoranda, joint committee reports, and collaborative task force communications. Informal horizontal channels include casual conversations among colleagues, shared social media groups, and hallway discussions. Both channels play important roles — formal channels ensure documentation and accountability, while informal channels often enable faster problem-solving and stronger interpersonal relationships (Obi & Chijioke, 2022).

Section 3

Forms and Functions of Horizontal Communication

Forms of Horizontal Communication

  • Interdepartmental Meetings: Scheduled gatherings where representatives from different departments at the same level discuss shared concerns, coordinate activities, or resolve issues.
  • Inter-departmental Memoranda: Written communications from one department to another, typically at the same hierarchical tier, requesting information, reporting progress, or seeking collaboration.
  • Joint Committee Reports: Formal documents produced by cross-departmental committees working together on a specific assignment.
  • Collaborative Emails and Digital Messages: Electronic communication between peers in different departments, increasingly conducted through platforms such as email, Microsoft Teams, or organisational WhatsApp groups.
  • Peer Consultation: Informal oral or written exchanges in which one department seeks the expertise or opinion of another at the same level.
  • Shared Project Documents: Co-authored reports, planning documents, or databases that departments contribute to and access jointly.

Functions of Horizontal Communication

  • Coordination: It allows departments with overlapping or interdependent responsibilities to synchronise their activities without routing every issue through vertical channels.
  • Problem-Solving: Peer-level communication facilitates the collaborative identification and resolution of operational problems that affect multiple departments.
  • Information Sharing: Departments share data, research findings, and operational updates that others need to perform their own functions effectively.
  • Conflict Resolution: Interdepartmental disputes are often more efficiently resolved through direct horizontal dialogue than through the time-consuming process of escalation up and then back down the hierarchy.
  • Social Cohesion and Morale: Horizontal communication builds workplace relationships, a sense of team identity, and collegial trust that strengthen overall organisational culture.
🏢 Workplace Example — Horizontal Communication

The Head of the Examinations Unit in a Nigerian university sends a memo to the Head of the ICT Department at the same level, requesting technical support for the deployment of an online examination portal. The two departments then hold a joint planning meeting to coordinate roles, timelines, and resource allocation. This is horizontal communication: two units at the same hierarchical level directly coordinating to achieve a common institutional goal, without routing the communication through the Registrar or Vice-Chancellor.

Strengths and Weaknesses of Horizontal Communication

✅ Strengths

  • Faster than routing through vertical channels
  • Builds interdepartmental relationships and trust
  • Reduces the burden on top management
  • Encourages collaborative problem-solving
  • Supports a culture of teamwork and shared ownership

❌ Weaknesses

  • Can lead to decisions made without management awareness
  • Risk of inter-departmental rivalry suppressing communication
  • May bypass formal accountability structures
  • Informal channels can spread rumours and misinformation
  • Difficult to coordinate in large or geographically spread organisations
🔑 Key Concept: Communication Silos — When Horizontal Flow Breaks Down

A communication silo occurs when departments or units within an organisation stop communicating effectively with one another, each becoming an isolated island of activity. Silos develop when there is departmental rivalry, distrust, poor leadership, or inadequate communication infrastructure. The result is duplication of effort, conflicting activities, missed opportunities for collaboration, and ultimately reduced organisational performance. Breaking down silos requires deliberate investment in horizontal communication channels and a culture of inter-departmental cooperation (Ihejirika & Obi, 2023).

Section 4

Understanding Diagonal Communication

Diagonal communication is the most distinctive and perhaps least discussed of the three major types of organisational communication flow. It occurs when an individual communicates with another person who is at a different hierarchical level and in a different department at the same time. In other words, diagonal communication cuts across both rank and functional boundaries simultaneously.

The concept of diagonal communication gained prominence with the rise of matrix organisations — structures in which employees report to both a functional head and a project manager — and cross-functional teams, where individuals from various departments and levels collaborate on specific projects. In these settings, a junior officer in one department may need to communicate directly with a senior officer in another — bypassing both their own line manager and the other person's departmental head (Adeyemi & Salami, 2022).

🔑 Key Concept: Matrix Organisations and Diagonal Communication

A matrix organisation is one in which employees have dual reporting lines — to a functional manager (based on their expertise, e.g. Finance) and to a project manager (based on their current assignment). This structure makes diagonal communication not merely possible but essential: a junior finance officer working on a health project may regularly communicate directly with a senior health administrator, creating a diagonal flow that the traditional hierarchy would not ordinarily permit (Chukwuemeka & Amadi, 2023).

Even in organisations that are not formally matrix-structured, diagonal communication occurs informally and increasingly through digital tools. An IT technician may email a faculty dean directly about a systems problem; a student welfare officer may contact an academic officer three levels above in a different faculty to resolve a student's case. These are all instances of diagonal communication — practical, efficient, and increasingly common in modern workplaces.

Section 5

Forms and Functions of Diagonal Communication

Forms of Diagonal Communication

  • Cross-Functional Task Forces and Committees: Groups drawn from different departments and hierarchical levels to work on a specific institutional challenge — where all members communicate with one another regardless of rank or departmental origin.
  • Matrix Team Communication: In matrix-structured organisations, employees communicate directly with project managers from other departments, creating regular diagonal exchanges.
  • Digital Cross-Level Messaging: Platforms such as email, enterprise messaging apps, and online collaboration tools have made it easy for individuals to communicate diagonally, bypassing the traditional requirement to route messages through intermediate levels.
  • Direct Consultations: A staff member from one department consulting directly with a senior officer in another department on a matter that requires specialist expertise.
  • Informal Diagonal Channels: Social interactions, informal conversations, and networking across departments and levels that generate information-sharing outside formal structures.

Functions of Diagonal Communication

  • Speed and Efficiency: Diagonal communication bypasses the slow, multi-level routing of vertical communication and the departmental insularity of horizontal communication, enabling faster information transfer and decision-making.
  • Innovation and Cross-Pollination: When people from different departments and levels interact directly, they bring different knowledge, perspectives, and skills — creating conditions for creative problem-solving and institutional innovation.
  • Flexibility: Diagonal communication is well-suited to dynamic, complex projects that require rapid adaptation — situations where the rigidity of vertical channels would be a liability.
  • Expertise Access: It allows individuals to access specialised knowledge in other departments without waiting for formal approval through the hierarchy.
  • Relationship Building Across Levels: Diagonal communication builds cross-level and cross-departmental relationships that enrich the organisation's social capital and informal networks.
🏢 Workplace Example — Diagonal Communication

A junior research officer in the Department of Mass Communication at a Nigerian university is working on a digital archiving project. She contacts the Deputy Registrar in the Registry directly — a senior officer in a completely different department — to seek guidance on archiving regulations. This communication cuts across both hierarchical level (junior officer to senior officer) and departmental boundary (academic department to administrative registry). It is diagonal: efficient, direct, and driven by the specific demands of the project rather than the formal hierarchy.

Strengths and Weaknesses of Diagonal Communication

✅ Strengths

  • Faster than vertical or horizontal routing for complex issues
  • Promotes innovation through diverse perspective-sharing
  • Reduces bureaucratic delays in cross-departmental collaboration
  • Enables expertise access across the organisation
  • Builds a networked, collaborative organisational culture

❌ Weaknesses

  • May be seen as bypassing proper authority channels
  • Can create confusion about accountability and responsibility
  • Risk of conflicting directives if not well managed
  • May breed resentment from bypassed supervisors
  • Difficult to sustain in highly bureaucratic institutions
🔑 Key Concept: The Grapevine and Informal Diagonal Networks

The grapevine is the informal network through which unverified information — rumours, speculation, and unofficial news — spreads rapidly across an organisation. The grapevine is inherently diagonal: it moves across departments and levels simultaneously, unbound by any formal structure. While it can be a useful channel for gauging employee sentiment, it also carries the risk of spreading inaccurate information that undermines trust and morale. Effective management of the grapevine requires robust formal communication — when official channels are clear and credible, the grapevine loses its fuel (Nwosu & Eze, 2022).

Section 6

Further Key Concepts in Horizontal and Diagonal Communication

Interdependence and the Need for Lateral Communication

Modern organisations are characterised by high degrees of functional interdependence — the extent to which one department's work depends on what another department does. The higher the interdependence, the greater the necessity for active horizontal and diagonal communication. Where interdependence is low, departments can operate relatively independently; where it is high, communication failures between departments directly translate into operational failures (Obi & Chijioke, 2022).

The Role of Trust in Lateral Communication

Horizontal and diagonal communication both depend heavily on interpersonal and inter-group trust. When departments view one another as competitors for resources, recognition, or influence, horizontal communication tends to be guarded, incomplete, or strategic rather than genuinely collaborative. Building trust across departments — through shared goals, transparent processes, and positive experiences of cooperation — is therefore a prerequisite for effective lateral communication (Adeyemi & Salami, 2022).

Technology as an Enabler of Diagonal Communication

Digital communication technologies have been among the most significant drivers of diagonal communication growth in modern organisations. Email, enterprise messaging platforms (such as Slack, Microsoft Teams, or Google Workspace), and collaborative project management tools all make it technically easy to communicate diagonally — reaching anyone in the organisation regardless of their department or hierarchical level. However, technology enables but does not guarantee quality diagonal communication; the culture and the relationships must also support it (Chukwuemeka & Amadi, 2023).

⚠️ Critical Note

The ease with which digital tools enable diagonal communication can be a double-edged sword. While it democratises access and speeds up information flow, it also bypasses formal structures in ways that can undermine authority, create confusion about decision-making, and generate information overload if not properly managed by organisational leadership.

Section 8

Factors Affecting Horizontal and Diagonal Communication

1. Departmental Rivalry and Competition

One of the most persistent barriers to effective horizontal communication is interdepartmental rivalry. When departments compete for budgets, recognition, or institutional prestige, they become reluctant to share information freely with their peers. This competitive dynamic creates communication silos and undermines the collaborative potential of horizontal channels. Diagonal communication suffers similarly when individuals fear that reaching across departmental and hierarchical lines will be perceived as overstepping or threatening to established power structures (Okafor, 2022).

2. Organisational Culture and Trust

Cultures that reward individual or departmental achievement over collective success tend to inhibit both horizontal and diagonal communication. Conversely, cultures built on shared values, mutual accountability, and collaborative norms provide the social foundation for rich lateral communication. Trust — between individuals, between departments, and across hierarchical levels — is the single most important cultural prerequisite for effective horizontal and diagonal communication (Eze & Nwachukwu, 2021).

3. Physical Distance and Remote Work

Horizontal and diagonal communication are facilitated by physical proximity — the ease of walking to a colleague's office, encountering them in a corridor, or attending shared meetings. When organisations are geographically dispersed, or when remote work increases distance between colleagues, horizontal and diagonal communication must be deliberately engineered rather than naturally occurring. This requires investment in digital infrastructure and intentional communication practices (Umoren & Uko, 2021).

4. Bureaucratic Structure and Formalism

Highly bureaucratic organisations — characterised by rigid rules, elaborate approval processes, and strict adherence to formal channels — tend to suppress both horizontal and diagonal communication. When every inter-departmental communication must be formally logged, approved, and routed through designated channels, the speed and spontaneity that make lateral communication valuable are lost (Adesanya & Ibrahim, 2023).

5. Digital Communication Tools

The availability and quality of digital communication infrastructure directly shapes lateral communication. Organisations with robust digital platforms — intranets, collaborative software, enterprise messaging — are better positioned to facilitate horizontal and diagonal communication than those relying on paper-based or face-to-face-only communication systems. The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated many organisations' adoption of digital communication tools, with significant implications for lateral communication patterns (Ihejirika & Obi, 2023).

Section 9

Horizontal and Diagonal Communication in Nigerian Organisations

Interdepartmental Dynamics in Nigerian Institutions

Nigerian organisations — both public and private — present a complex environment for horizontal and diagonal communication. The traditional emphasis on hierarchy and authority in Nigerian institutional culture often makes lateral communication appear unconventional or even inappropriate. In many public institutions, the norm is to route all interdepartmental communication through the head of department, the head of the institution, and back down — a process that is both slow and prone to distortion (Obi & Chijioke, 2022).

Horizontal communication does occur, however — often informally. In Nigerian universities, for example, heads of academic departments routinely coordinate directly on matters such as timetabling, resource sharing, and joint examination committees. In the private sector, peer-level managers in functional areas such as sales, operations, and finance communicate laterally on a daily basis. The challenge is that these horizontal communications are often underdocumented and under-institutionalised (Adeyemi & Salami, 2022).

Challenges Specific to Nigerian Contexts

Several factors complicate horizontal and diagonal communication in Nigerian organisations. Ethnic and tribal affiliations sometimes create informal loyalty networks that supersede departmental or institutional communication channels — determining who communicates with whom, and on what terms. Departmental politics and competition for limited resources can generate tensions that inhibit collaborative lateral communication. Poor communication infrastructure in many public institutions — inadequate internet, unreliable electricity, and absence of digital collaboration platforms — further limits the potential of both horizontal and diagonal channels (Nwosu & Eze, 2022).

🏢 Nigerian Organisational Context

In a Nigerian government ministry, a project implementation unit working on a cross-sectoral development programme routinely communicates directly with officers from the finance, planning, and monitoring departments — all at different levels and from different functional areas. This matrix-like arrangement, driven by project necessity, generates significant diagonal communication. However, it also creates friction with traditional hierarchical norms, as bypassed heads of department sometimes feel their authority has been undermined.

Emerging Improvements in Nigerian Workplaces

The increasing adoption of digital communication tools — particularly WhatsApp groups, email, and video conferencing platforms — has significantly expanded the scope of both horizontal and diagonal communication in Nigerian organisations. Cross-functional WhatsApp groups in which officers from different departments and levels coordinate project activities are now common in both the public and private sectors. Nigerian universities increasingly use joint faculty committees and interdisciplinary research teams, creating structured forums for horizontal communication. These developments reflect a gradual but real evolution in Nigerian organisational communication culture (Chukwuemeka & Amadi, 2023).

Section 10

Impact on Organisational Success and Failure

When Horizontal and Diagonal Communication Work Well

Organisations that sustain effective horizontal and diagonal communication enjoy a range of measurable benefits. Coordination between departments is smoother, reducing duplication of effort and operational conflicts. Problem-solving is faster and more creative, as diverse perspectives are brought to bear. Innovation flourishes when individuals from different functions and levels can exchange ideas directly. Staff morale improves because people feel connected to colleagues across the organisation, rather than isolated within departmental silos. Management is freed from the burden of mediating every interdepartmental interaction, enabling top leadership to focus on strategic matters (Okafor, 2022).

When These Communication Forms Fail

The consequences of poor horizontal and diagonal communication are equally significant. Departments that do not communicate effectively with peers tend to work at cross-purposes, pursue contradictory goals, or duplicate efforts at significant cost. In the absence of diagonal communication, complex cross-functional projects stall because information cannot move efficiently across the levels and departments involved. Communication silos — the product of failed lateral communication — are a leading cause of organisational inefficiency, poor service delivery, and institutional decline (Ihejirika & Obi, 2023).

⚠️ Reflection Point

Consider a hospital in which the medical, pharmacy, and administrative departments do not communicate horizontally. A patient's prescription cannot be filled because the pharmacy was not informed of a supply change communicated only within the medical department. This failure of horizontal communication — a silo — has a direct, measurable impact on the people the organisation exists to serve.

Strategies for Improving Horizontal and Diagonal Communication

  • Establishing formal inter-departmental liaison roles responsible for maintaining regular communication across units
  • Creating cross-functional committees and task forces that bring together staff from different departments and levels
  • Investing in digital collaboration platforms that enable lateral communication across geographical and hierarchical boundaries
  • Designing shared physical spaces — common rooms, collaborative working areas — that naturally encourage lateral interaction
  • Fostering an organisational culture that values collaborative achievement over departmental competition
  • Providing training in interpersonal and cross-functional communication skills
  • Recognising and rewarding successful interdepartmental collaboration at the institutional level
