1. Introduction

Communication is the lifeblood of every organisation. Without it, no instruction can be given, no decision can be coordinated, no policy can be implemented, and no relationship can be sustained. Whether we are speaking of a small family-owned enterprise, a government ministry, a multinational corporation, or a non-governmental organisation, the ability to transmit meaning accurately and efficiently between people is the single most important process that holds the institution together and enables it to pursue its goals.

Yet despite its obvious importance, communication within organisations is frequently taken for granted. Many managers assume that because a message has been sent, it has been received and understood. Many employees assume that because they have spoken, they have communicated. These assumptions are dangerous. Research consistently demonstrates that communication breakdowns are among the most common causes of organisational inefficiency, conflict, low morale, and strategic failure. Verčič and Špoljarić (2020) found that the quality of internal communication is a stronger predictor of employee satisfaction than either compensation or physical working conditions — a finding that underscores how central communication is to the everyday experience of organisational life.

This lecture serves as the foundation for the entire Organisational Communication series. It introduces the core concepts, definitions, models, and principles that will be developed in greater depth across the nine topics that follow. Students are encouraged to engage with each concept carefully, as the ideas introduced here will recur throughout the course.

2. Defining Communication

Before we can understand organisational communication, we must first understand what communication itself means. The word "communication" comes from the Latin communicare, meaning "to share" or "to make common." At its most fundamental level, communication is the process by which one party (the sender) transmits meaning to another party (the receiver) through the use of shared symbols — primarily language, but also gestures, images, sounds, and other signs.

Key Concept — Communication Defined

Communication is the process of creating, transmitting, receiving, and interpreting messages between two or more parties in order to share meaning. It is not merely the act of sending a message — it is the entire cycle through which meaning is constructed by the sender, encoded into a transmittable form, delivered through a channel, decoded by the receiver, and confirmed through feedback.

Several important points follow from this definition. First, communication is a process, not a single event. It involves multiple stages, each of which can succeed or fail. Second, communication requires shared meaning — if the receiver does not understand the message as the sender intended it, communication has not truly occurred, even though a message was transmitted. Third, communication is two-directional: it involves not only sending but also receiving, interpreting, and responding. Without feedback, the sender has no way of knowing whether the message was understood.

It is also important to recognise that communication can be intentional or unintentional. A manager who raises her voice during a meeting is communicating frustration, whether or not she means to. An employee who arrives late to work every day is communicating something about his attitude, even if he never says a word about it. This means that in organisations, people are always communicating — through their words, their actions, their silences, and their patterns of behaviour. Neuberger, Lemos, and Watkins (2023) emphasised that effective communicators are those who are aware not only of the messages they intend to send but also of the unintended messages their behaviour may convey.

3. Defining Organisational Communication

Organisational communication is a specialised field of study that examines how communication functions within the unique context of formal organisations. While general communication theory applies to all human interaction — conversations between friends, family discussions, public speeches — organisational communication focuses specifically on the processes, structures, channels, and cultures through which messages flow within and between organisations.

Key Concept — Organisational Communication Defined

Organisational communication is the process by which individuals and groups within an organisation create, exchange, interpret, and use information to coordinate their activities, make decisions, achieve shared goals, and maintain the social fabric of the organisation. It encompasses all forms of message exchange — verbal (spoken and written), non-verbal (body language, gestures, spatial arrangements), formal (through official channels), and informal (through social networks and personal relationships).

What distinguishes organisational communication from general communication is its context. In an organisation, communication does not happen in a vacuum. It is shaped by the organisation's structure (who reports to whom), its culture (what norms govern how people speak and listen), its technology (what channels are available), its policies (what must be communicated formally versus informally), and its power dynamics (who has the authority to communicate what, and to whom). Understanding organisational communication therefore requires understanding the organisation itself.

Men and Yue (2024) argued that organisational communication should be understood not merely as the transmission of information but as the primary mechanism through which organisational reality is constructed. Through communication, leaders define the organisation's mission and vision, managers translate strategy into action, employees coordinate their tasks, and the organisation as a whole presents itself to the outside world. In this sense, communication is not something that happens within an organisation — it is the process through which the organisation comes into being and sustains itself over time.

Key Concept — How Organisational Communication Differs from General Communication

While all communication involves the exchange of meaning between parties, organisational communication has several distinctive features:

1. Goal-oriented: Communication in organisations is directed towards achieving specific objectives — completing a project, implementing a policy, resolving a dispute, or motivating employees.

2. Structurally constrained: Messages must often travel through established channels and hierarchies. A front-line worker cannot typically bypass three levels of management to speak directly to the CEO.

3. Role-based: People communicate not only as individuals but as occupants of roles — manager, subordinate, colleague, representative — each carrying expectations about what and how they should communicate.

4. Culturally embedded: Every organisation develops its own communication culture — unwritten rules about what is acceptable to say, how formally or informally to speak, and how much dissent is tolerated.

5. Multi-channel: Organisations use a wide variety of channels simultaneously — face-to-face, email, memos, meetings, video calls, instant messaging, notice boards, and more — and selecting the right channel is itself a communication skill.

4. The Communication Process

Understanding how communication works requires a clear model of the process through which messages are created, transmitted, received, and interpreted. While numerous models have been proposed over the decades, the most widely used framework in organisational studies identifies seven core elements that are present in every act of communication.

Key Concept — The Seven Elements of the Communication Process

1. Sender (Source): The person or group who initiates the communication by creating a message. The sender has an idea, thought, instruction, or feeling that they wish to share with another party. The effectiveness of communication begins with the sender's clarity of thought — if the sender is unclear about what they want to say, the message will inevitably be confused.

2. Encoding: The process by which the sender converts their idea into a transmittable form — words, sentences, diagrams, gestures, or symbols. Encoding involves choosing the right language, tone, level of formality, and structure for the intended audience. A manager encoding an instruction for a team of engineers will use different language than when encoding the same information for a board of directors. Poor encoding — using jargon, ambiguous language, or an inappropriate tone — is one of the most common sources of communication failure.

3. Message: The actual content that is transmitted — the words spoken, the text written, the diagram drawn, or the gesture made. The message is the tangible product of the encoding process. It can be simple ("The meeting is at 2pm") or complex ("Here is our revised five-year strategic plan"). The message carries both content (the factual information) and relational meaning (what the message implies about the relationship between sender and receiver — respect, authority, friendliness, urgency).

4. Channel (Medium): The pathway through which the message travels from sender to receiver. Channels include face-to-face conversation, telephone calls, video conferences, emails, memos, reports, instant messages, notice boards, and social media platforms. The choice of channel significantly affects how the message is received. Ishii, Lyons, and Carr (2020) reaffirmed that "rich" channels (face-to-face, video) are best for complex or sensitive messages, while "lean" channels (email, memo) are sufficient for routine factual information.

5. Decoding: The process by which the receiver interprets the message and assigns meaning to it. Decoding is the mirror image of encoding — the receiver must translate the words, symbols, or gestures back into an idea or understanding. Effective communication requires that the receiver's decoding matches the sender's encoding as closely as possible. However, decoding is influenced by the receiver's education, experience, cultural background, emotional state, and relationship with the sender, which means that misinterpretation is always a possibility.

6. Receiver: The person or group for whom the message is intended. The receiver's role is not passive — effective communication requires the receiver to listen attentively, read carefully, ask clarifying questions, and provide feedback. A message that is technically well-encoded and transmitted through an appropriate channel can still fail if the receiver is distracted, disinterested, or unable to decode it.

7. Feedback: The receiver's response to the message, which completes the communication cycle. Feedback tells the sender whether the message was received, understood, and accepted — or whether it was missed, misunderstood, or rejected. Feedback can be verbal ("I understand, I'll have the report ready by Friday"), non-verbal (a nod, a confused expression), or behavioural (the receiver does or does not perform the requested action). Without feedback, communication is one-directional and unreliable.

Key Concept — Noise in the Communication Process

Noise refers to any factor that distorts, disrupts, or interferes with the transmission or reception of a message at any stage of the communication process. Noise can be:

Physical noise: Environmental disturbances such as loud machinery, poor phone connections, background conversations, or uncomfortable room temperatures that make it difficult to hear, concentrate, or read.

Semantic noise: Language-related interference such as jargon, ambiguous words, cultural idioms, or technical terminology that the receiver does not understand.

Psychological noise: Internal mental and emotional states — anxiety, prejudice, distrust, boredom, anger — that distort how the sender encodes or the receiver decodes a message.

Organisational noise: Structural factors such as information overload, rigid hierarchies, departmental silos, and filtering by intermediaries that impede the flow of messages within the organisation.

Effective communicators are those who anticipate potential sources of noise and take steps to minimise them — by choosing quiet settings, using clear language, checking for understanding, and being attentive to the receiver's emotional state.

Workplace Example — The Communication Process in Action

A department head (sender) wants to inform her team about a change in the submission deadline for monthly reports. She decides that the change is important enough to communicate both orally and in writing. She encodes the message by composing a clear, concise email stating the new deadline, the reason for the change, and what is expected. The message is the email itself. The channel is the company email system. Each team member (receiver) reads and decodes the message. One team member replies to confirm he understands (verbal feedback); another simply submits his report by the new date (behavioural feedback). However, one team member was on leave when the email was sent, did not see it, and missed the deadline. The noise in this case was the employee's absence — a practical barrier that prevented the message from reaching its intended recipient. To address this, the department head could have followed up with a brief oral reminder at the next team meeting.

5. Importance of Communication in Organisations

Communication is not merely one of many organisational functions — it is the function that makes all other functions possible. Without effective communication, planning cannot be translated into action, decisions cannot be implemented, employees cannot be coordinated, and stakeholders cannot be informed. The following points explain why communication is critically important to every organisation.

Key Concept — Why Communication Matters

1. Coordination of activities: Organisations consist of multiple individuals, teams, and departments, each performing different tasks that must fit together to achieve a common goal. Communication is the mechanism through which these activities are coordinated. Without clear instructions, schedules, updates, and progress reports flowing between units, duplication of effort, missed deadlines, and conflicting actions become inevitable. Mroz, Allen, Verhoeven, and Shuffler (2023) demonstrated that teams with well-structured communication processes consistently outperform those that rely on ad hoc or uncoordinated exchanges.

2. Decision-making: Every organisational decision — from routine operational choices to major strategic initiatives — depends on information. Communication is the process through which relevant information reaches the decision-maker, alternatives are discussed, and the final decision is communicated to those who must implement it. Poor communication leads to decisions based on incomplete, outdated, or distorted information, which in turn leads to poor outcomes.

3. Employee motivation and morale: Employees who are well-informed about the organisation's goals, their role in achieving those goals, and how their performance is evaluated tend to be more motivated, engaged, and productive. Yue, Men, and Ferguson (2021) found that transparent communication from leadership is one of the strongest predictors of employee organisational identification — the sense of belonging and commitment that drives voluntary effort and loyalty.

4. Conflict resolution: Conflict is inevitable in organisations where people with different personalities, interests, and perspectives work together under pressure. Communication is the primary tool for managing and resolving conflict — through dialogue, negotiation, mediation, and clarification of misunderstandings. Organisations that suppress communication or discourage open discussion tend to accumulate unresolved grievances that eventually erupt into more serious problems.

5. Building organisational culture and identity: The way people communicate within an organisation — what they say, how they say it, what topics are encouraged or avoided, how disagreements are handled — shapes the organisation's culture over time. Communication is the medium through which values are expressed, norms are established, stories are shared, and a collective sense of identity is formed. Men and Yue (2024) stressed that leaders who communicate transparently and consistently play a decisive role in building a culture of trust and accountability.

6. External relations and reputation: Organisations do not exist in isolation. They must communicate effectively with customers, suppliers, investors, regulators, media, and the wider public. External communication shapes the organisation's reputation, brand image, and stakeholder relationships. A single poorly worded press release, a dismissive response to a customer complaint, or a failure to communicate during a crisis can cause lasting reputational damage.

Workplace Example — When Communication Fails

Consider a medium-sized manufacturing company that introduces a new shift rotation system. Management announces the change via a brief memo pinned to the notice board in the staff canteen. Many workers do not see the memo because they eat lunch at their workstations. Those who do see it are confused because the memo uses abbreviations and references to policy numbers without explanation. No opportunity is given for questions or feedback. The result: widespread confusion, resentment, absenteeism on the wrong shifts, and a significant drop in morale. The policy itself may have been sound, but the failure to communicate it properly — through the right channels, with adequate clarity, and with provision for feedback — caused it to fail in practice. This example illustrates that what is communicated matters, but how it is communicated matters equally.

6. Characteristics of Effective Organisational Communication

Not all communication is equally effective. A message can be transmitted without being received, received without being understood, or understood without being acted upon. For communication to achieve its purpose within an organisation, it must possess certain characteristics. Understanding these characteristics helps students evaluate the quality of communication in any workplace setting and identify areas for improvement.

Key Concept — The Seven Characteristics (The 7 Cs)

1. Clarity: The message should be expressed in language that the receiver can easily understand. Ambiguity, vague terms, and unnecessarily complex sentences reduce clarity. A clear message uses simple, direct language, avoids jargon (unless the audience shares that vocabulary), and states its purpose upfront. The test of clarity is not whether the sender understands the message, but whether the receiver does.

2. Completeness: The message should contain all the information the receiver needs to understand the situation and take appropriate action. Incomplete messages force receivers to guess, assume, or seek additional information — all of which create delays and increase the risk of error. A complete message answers the fundamental questions: What? Who? When? Where? Why? How?

3. Conciseness: While completeness is important, the message should not contain unnecessary detail, repetition, or filler. Conciseness means saying what needs to be said in the fewest words possible without sacrificing clarity or completeness. In an era of information overload, concise communication respects the receiver's time and attention. Welch (2022) reported that employees are more likely to read and act upon short, focused messages than lengthy, unfocused ones.

4. Correctness: The information in the message should be accurate, factual, and free from errors — including factual errors, grammatical errors, and incorrect data. Incorrect information can lead to flawed decisions, wasted resources, and loss of credibility. The sender has a responsibility to verify facts before communicating them.

5. Courtesy: The message should be respectful, considerate, and sensitive to the receiver's feelings, status, and perspective. Courteous communication builds goodwill, strengthens relationships, and makes the receiver more receptive to the message. Discourteous communication — sarcasm, condescension, blame — creates defensiveness and resistance, even when the underlying message is valid.

6. Concreteness: The message should use specific, definite facts and figures rather than vague, abstract generalisations. Instead of "We need to improve productivity soon," a concrete message would state "We need to increase output by 15% before the end of the third quarter." Concrete communication reduces ambiguity and gives the receiver a clear basis for action.

7. Consideration: The sender should compose the message with the receiver's needs, background, and perspective in mind. This means adapting language, tone, channel, and level of detail to suit the audience. A technical report written for engineers should be different in style and vocabulary from a summary written for the board of directors, even though both cover the same topic. Ruck and Welch (2022) emphasised that audience-centred communication is a hallmark of effective organisational communicators.

7. Scope and Dimensions of Organisational Communication

Organisational communication is not a single, uniform activity. It operates across multiple dimensions, each with its own characteristics, purposes, and challenges. Students must understand these dimensions to appreciate the full complexity of communication in organisational life.

7.1 Internal vs External Communication

Key Concept — Internal Communication

Internal communication refers to all message exchanges that occur within the boundaries of the organisation — between employees, teams, departments, and levels of management. Its primary purposes are coordination, information sharing, decision-making, motivation, and the maintenance of organisational culture. Examples include staff meetings, internal memos, company newsletters, intranet postings, departmental emails, and informal conversations between colleagues.

Internal communication is critical because it determines how well the organisation functions on a day-to-day basis. Verčič and Špoljarić (2020) demonstrated that organisations with strong internal communication practices report higher employee engagement, lower turnover, and better operational performance than those where internal communication is weak or neglected.

Key Concept — External Communication

External communication refers to all message exchanges between the organisation and parties outside it — customers, suppliers, investors, regulators, media, competitors, and the general public. Its primary purposes are marketing, public relations, stakeholder management, regulatory compliance, and reputation building. Examples include press releases, advertising, customer service interactions, annual reports, investor briefings, and social media presence.

External communication shapes how the outside world perceives the organisation. A consistent, professional, and transparent external communication strategy builds trust and credibility; an inconsistent, evasive, or dishonest one destroys them. Neuberger, Lemos, and Watkins (2023) noted that in the age of social media, external communication failures can become public instantly and cause reputational damage that takes years to repair.

7.2 Formal vs Informal Communication

Key Concept — Formal Communication

Formal communication follows the officially established channels, structures, and protocols of the organisation. It flows along the lines of the organisational chart — downward from superiors to subordinates, upward from subordinates to superiors, and horizontally between peers at the same level. Examples include official memos, policy directives, performance appraisals, board resolutions, and minuted meetings. Formal communication is characterised by its documentation, its adherence to protocol, and its institutional authority.

Key Concept — Informal Communication (The Grapevine)

Informal communication occurs outside the official channels — through personal conversations, social interactions, rumours, and the network of personal relationships that exist alongside the formal structure. This network is often called the grapevine. Informal communication is faster, more flexible, and often more emotionally expressive than formal communication. It can be a valuable source of information, social support, and organisational intelligence.

However, the grapevine also carries risks. Because it is unregulated and unverified, informal communication can spread rumours, misinformation, and anxiety — particularly during periods of uncertainty such as restructuring, mergers, or leadership transitions. Mbarushimana and Allida (2022) observed that managers who ignore the grapevine do so at their peril: rather than trying to suppress informal communication, effective leaders monitor it, correct inaccuracies promptly, and use formal channels to provide the accurate information that the grapevine is trying (and often failing) to supply.

7.3 Directional Flows of Communication

Communication within organisations flows in several directions, each serving a distinct purpose. These directional flows will be examined in greater depth in Topics 4 and 5 of this series, but a brief introduction is essential here:

Key Concept — Directional Flows (Overview)

Downward communication: Messages flowing from higher levels to lower levels of the hierarchy — instructions, policies, feedback, and announcements from management to employees.

Upward communication: Messages flowing from lower levels to higher levels — reports, suggestions, complaints, and feedback from employees to management.

Horizontal communication: Messages exchanged between individuals or units at the same hierarchical level — coordination between departments, peer-to-peer collaboration, and cross-functional teamwork.

Diagonal communication: Messages that cross both hierarchical levels and departmental boundaries simultaneously — for example, a junior accountant communicating directly with a senior marketing manager about a budget issue. Diagonal communication is becoming increasingly common in modern organisations that value speed and flexibility over rigid adherence to the chain of command.

8. The Role of Communication in Organisational Success and Failure

The difference between organisations that thrive and those that struggle can often be traced to the quality of their communication. Research across industries and sectors consistently shows that effective communication is a competitive advantage, while poor communication is a recurring source of failure.

Workplace Example — Communication Driving Success

A technology company preparing to launch a new product establishes a cross-functional communication task force that includes representatives from engineering, marketing, sales, customer support, and finance. The task force meets weekly, shares progress reports via a shared digital platform, and escalates issues promptly through clearly defined channels. As a result, the product launches on schedule, marketing materials accurately reflect the product's features, the sales team is fully trained, and customer support is prepared for common queries. The success of the launch is not due to any single department's brilliance — it is the result of effective coordination achieved through structured, transparent communication.

Workplace Example — Communication Driving Failure

A hospital experiences a serious medication error when a doctor's handwritten prescription is misread by a pharmacist, and the nurse administering the medication does not verify the dosage with the prescribing doctor because "she didn't want to bother him." The error results from multiple communication failures: an unclear message (illegible handwriting), an inadequate channel (handwritten notes instead of electronic prescriptions), and a cultural barrier (the nurse's reluctance to question a senior colleague). Investigations into organisational failures — from industrial accidents to financial scandals — repeatedly reveal that the root cause is not a lack of technical knowledge but a breakdown in communication. Oduaran and Okojie (2023) stressed that organisations must invest in communication systems, training, and culture just as deliberately as they invest in technology, equipment, and human capital.

The lesson for students is clear: communication competence is not a "soft skill" that can be left to chance. It is a core organisational capability that must be studied, practised, evaluated, and continuously improved. The remaining nine topics in this series will equip you with the specific knowledge and analytical tools needed to understand and improve communication in any organisational setting.

9. Examination Practice Questions

Examination Practice — Attempt All Questions

Answer in clear, well-structured prose with relevant examples where appropriate. Marks are allocated to each question as indicated.

  1. Discuss the concept of communication. In your answer, explain what communication means, identify its key characteristics, and distinguish it from mere information transmission. (15 marks)
  2. Discuss the concept of organisational communication and explain how it differs from general everyday communication. Illustrate your answer with at least two relevant workplace examples. (15 marks)
  3. Enumerate and discuss the seven elements of the communication process. For each element, provide a clear explanation and a practical example drawn from an organisational setting. (25 marks)
  4. Noise is widely regarded as the greatest enemy of effective communication. List and discuss four types of noise that can disrupt the communication process in an organisation. For each type, provide a workplace example and suggest one strategy for minimising its impact. (20 marks)
  5. Enumerate and discuss six reasons why communication is important to organisational performance. Support each point with a relevant example. (20 marks)
  6. The 7 Cs of effective communication are widely taught as a framework for evaluating communication quality. List and discuss any five of these characteristics, explaining how each contributes to effective organisational communication. (20 marks)
  7. Discuss the distinction between formal and informal communication in organisations. In your opinion, should managers attempt to control or suppress the grapevine? Justify your position with at least two well-developed arguments. (15 marks)
  8. Internal and external communication serve different but equally important purposes in organisational life. Discuss the meaning and purpose of each, providing at least two examples of each type. (15 marks)
  9. In your opinion, can an organisation achieve its goals without effective communication? With relevant examples, justify your position with at least three well-developed arguments. (15 marks)
  10. Poor communication is frequently cited as a leading cause of organisational failure. Discuss this statement with reference to at least two real-world or hypothetical examples, explaining how communication breakdowns contributed to negative outcomes and what could have been done differently. (20 marks)
